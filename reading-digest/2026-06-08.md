## 1. SubtleMemory: A Benchmark for Fine-Grained Relational Memory Discrimination in Long-Horizon AI Agents
- **Authors:** Wenxuan Wang, Haoyu Sun, Fukuan Hou, et al.
- **Summary:** The authors construct a benchmark that stresses how well long-horizon agents can keep track of, and disambiguate, fine-grained relational facts that accumulate across many interaction turns. Rather than testing single-fact recall, the suite forces the agent to distinguish near-duplicate relations (same entities, slightly different roles) — a setting where naive context windows and RAG stores tend to collapse. Reported results expose a large gap between current LLM-agent memory designs and the discrimination accuracy required for sustained dialog.
- **Theme:** Memory for LLMs and agents
- **Relevance note:** Directly targets the hardest open problem in episodic/relational agent memory, with a benchmark others can adopt — highest signal of any paper this batch.
- **Link:** https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.05761

## 2. M³Exam: Benchmarking Multimodal Memory for Realistic User-Agent Interactions
- **Authors:** Zhengjun Huang, Wenxuan Liu, Zhoujin Tian, et al.
- **Summary:** M³Exam evaluates how agents recall facts that were originally introduced as images, audio, or interleaved modalities across long user sessions. It is query-centric: each test probes a specific multimodal recall, and a companion procedure ("M³Proctor") diagnoses which modality the agent is over- or under-trusting at retrieval time. Findings indicate that current multimodal agents over-rely on the textual channel even when the answer was originally presented visually.
- **Theme:** Memory for LLMs and agents
- **Relevance note:** Extends the agent-memory frontier into the multimodal regime that other memory benchmarks ignore, and surfaces a concrete failure mode (modality bias) to attack.
- **Link:** https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.07402

## 3. MemDreamer: Decoupling Perception and Reasoning for Long Video Understanding
- **Authors:** Cong Chen, Guo Gan, Kaixiang Ji, et al.
- **Summary:** MemDreamer splits long-video MLLMs into a perception pass that compresses frames into a hierarchical graph memory and a reasoning pass that issues agentic retrievals over that memory at query time. By replacing the usual flat token soup with a structured store, it reduces the context the reasoning model has to attend over while keeping the salient facts addressable. The authors report comparable or better long-video QA at a fraction of the active-context length.
- **Theme:** Memory for LLMs and agents
- **Relevance note:** Concrete architecture for retrieval-augmented memory in a long-horizon perceptual setting; the hierarchical-graph + agentic-retriever split generalizes beyond video.
- **Link:** https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.07512

## 4. Agentopia: Long-Term Life Simulation and Learning in Agent Societies
- **Authors:** Xintao Wang, Sirui Zheng, Hongqiu Wu, et al.
- **Summary:** Agentopia runs 100 LLM agents through ten simulated years of social life, with a "life reward" objective that ties policy updates to the experiences agents accumulate. The framework tests whether sustained multi-agent interaction, rather than supervised data, can move base LLMs toward more anthropomorphic social capabilities. Reported probes show measurable shifts in agents' planning, role behavior, and reciprocity over the simulation.
- **Theme:** Multi-agent frameworks & orchestration
- **Relevance note:** One of the largest published long-horizon multi-agent training loops; directly relevant to society-of-agents and emergent-coordination research.
- **Link:** https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.07513

## 5. Modelling Opinion Dynamics at Scale with Deep MARL
- **Authors:** Lukas Seier, Brandon Kaplowitz, Sebastian Towers, et al.
- **Summary:** The paper scales deep multi-agent reinforcement learning to populations of up to ~1000 interacting agents and uses it to reproduce known opinion-dynamics phenomena (polarization, consensus collapse, echo-chamber formation). The authors design a shared-policy / centralized-critic stack that keeps training tractable as population grows, and validate against analytical opinion-dynamics models. Results connect MARL design choices to specific population-level outcomes.
- **Theme:** Multi-agent frameworks & orchestration
- **Relevance note:** Rare empirical look at *scale* in MARL plus a credible bridge to social-science models — useful template for stress-testing multi-agent coordination.
- **Link:** https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.07487

## 6. OpenSkill: Open-World Self-Evolution for LLM Agents
- **Authors:** Zhiling Yan, Dingjie Song, Hanrong Zhang, et al.
- **Summary:** OpenSkill lets an LLM agent grow its own skill library and verification signals from open-world resources, with no task-specific supervision. The agent proposes candidate skills, executes them against open-web tools, and bootstraps verifiers from observed outcomes, accumulating a reusable skill set over time. The authors show transfer to held-out tasks the system never explicitly trained for.
- **Theme:** Multi-agent frameworks & orchestration (single-agent self-evolution, but a building block for orchestrated agents)
- **Relevance note:** Pushes on the self-improvement loop that orchestration frameworks increasingly depend on; the verifier-bootstrapping piece is the novel bit.
- **Link:** https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.06741

## 7. When Tools Fail: Benchmarking Dynamic Replanning and Anomaly Recovery in LLM Agents (ToolMaze)
- **Authors:** Dongsheng Zhu, Xuchen Ma, Yucheng Shen, et al.
- **Summary:** ToolMaze is a benchmark that deliberately injects realistic tool failures — timeouts, malformed outputs, and silent semantic errors — into otherwise-standard tool-using agent tasks. The authors evaluate replanning and recovery, and report that current Tool-Integrated Reasoning agents degrade most sharply not on hard errors but on *semantic* failures where the tool returns plausible-looking wrong output. This gives a concrete failure taxonomy for agent robustness.
- **Theme:** Multi-agent frameworks & orchestration (tool-use / replanning subsystem)
- **Relevance note:** The semantic-failure finding is actionable for any orchestration framework that assumes tool outputs are trustworthy — a likely template for robustness evaluation.
- **Link:** https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.05806

## 8. Socratic-SWE: Self-Evolving Coding Agents via Trace-Derived Agent Skills
- **Authors:** Chuan Xiao, Zhengbo Jiao, Shaobo Wang, et al.
- **Summary:** Socratic-SWE mines historical solve traces from a software-engineering agent and distills them into reusable "agent skills," then automatically generates targeted repair tasks that exercise weaknesses found in the traces. The loop closes when the agent re-trains on its own successful skill applications. The authors report compounding gains on SWE-style benchmarks as the skill library grows.
- **Theme:** Core LLM advances directly relevant to agents (self-improvement / skill libraries)
- **Relevance note:** Trace-derived skill libraries are a clean, generalizable mechanism for continual improvement — applies beyond SWE.
- **Link:** https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.07412

## 9. Thinking with Imagination: Agentic Visual Spatial Reasoning with World Simulators (Astra)
- **Authors:** Chenming Zhu, Jingli Lin, Yilin Long, et al.
- **Summary:** Astra equips a vision-language model with an action-conditioned world simulator that it can call as a mental "what-if" tool during spatial reasoning. Rather than reasoning purely over the input image, the VLM proposes candidate actions, asks the simulator to imagine the resulting view, and conditions its next step on that imagined frame. Reported gains on spatial reasoning benchmarks come from this explicit imagination loop.
- **Theme:** Core LLM advances directly relevant to agents (planning / reasoning)
- **Relevance note:** Couples planning and reasoning through a learned simulator — a pattern that is starting to recur across agentic-VLM work and worth tracking.
- **Link:** https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.06476

## 10. Act As a Real Researcher: AARRI-Bench
- **Authors:** Jiayu Wang, Weijiang Lv, Bowen Fu, et al.
- **Summary:** AARRI-Bench evaluates LLM agents on fine-grained research subtasks (literature triage, hypothesis articulation, experiment-design critique) rather than end-to-end "do science" prompts. Each axis is scored independently, and the authors show that agents that look competent on aggregated benchmarks fail on specific judgment-heavy axes such as identifying confounders. The result is a calibrated picture of where research agents actually stand.
- **Theme:** Core LLM advances directly relevant to agents (agentic-system evaluation)
- **Relevance note:** Fine-grained axes are more diagnostic than aggregate scores; useful for anyone benchmarking research/scientific agents.
- **Link:** https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.07462
