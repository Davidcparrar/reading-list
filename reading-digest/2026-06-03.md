## 1. Language Models Need Sleep: Learning to Self-Modify and Consolidate Memories
**Authors:** Ali Behrouz, Farnoosh Hashemi, Vahab Mirrokni (Google)

**Summary:** The authors borrow a biology-inspired two-phase "sleep" loop and bolt it onto LLM training so that short-term, in-context experience can actually become long-term parameter knowledge instead of being thrown away at the end of a session. Phase one ("Knowledge Seeding") is an upward distillation that consolidates a smaller self's memories into a larger network via a hybrid of on-policy distillation and RL-style imitation learning; phase two ("Dreaming") is a self-improvement loop in which the model uses RL to generate its own synthetic curriculum to rehearse new skills without human supervision. Reported gains on long-horizon, continual-learning, knowledge-incorporation, and few-shot generalization tasks suggest the consolidation step — not just more data — is what's missing.

**Theme:** 1 — Memory for LLMs (long-term consolidation; the entire architectural pitch is about turning ephemeral context into durable parameter memory).

**Relevance:** Directly on Theme 1 and unusually concrete about *how* to consolidate, not just *that* you should. A Google paper that combines memory consolidation with an RL self-improvement loop is exactly the kind of in-scope methods contribution this digest prioritizes.

**Link:** https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.03979

## 2. Value-Aware Stochastic KV Cache Eviction for Reasoning Models
**Authors:** Ting-Yun Chang, Harvey Yiyun Fu, Deqing Fu (USC)

**Summary:** The paper diagnoses two specific failure modes of existing KV-cache eviction for long-CoT reasoning models — a small set of value states have abnormally large magnitudes and evicting them collapses the model into repetition loops, and deterministic eviction reduces cache diversity in a way that hurts accuracy — and proposes VaSE, a training-free recipe that protects large-magnitude values and adds stochasticity to the eviction decision. With 4× compression on Qwen3 reasoning models, VaSE beats the strongest prior eviction baseline by >4 points on average across six reasoning benchmarks and also beats selection-based sparse-attention methods at matched sparsity. It is FlashAttention2-compatible and gives reasoning models a static memory footprint, which is the actually-deployable framing.

**Theme:** 1 — Memory for LLMs (working-memory / context-state management; KV-cache as the working memory of a reasoning model).

**Relevance:** A second Theme-1 paper but at a very different altitude from #1 — instead of building new long-term memory, it asks how to *manage* the working-memory state a reasoning model already uses, with a concrete mechanism (value-magnitude protection + stochastic eviction) and clean empirical wins. Pairs well with #1 to cover both ends of the memory stack.

**Link:** https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.03928

## 3. Ψ-Bench: Evaluating Persona-Sensitive Influencing in Persuasive Dialogues
**Authors:** Peixuan Han, Hongyi Du, Jiayu Liu, et al.

**Summary:** Existing personalized-agent work treats the LLM as a passive responder to user preferences; Ψ-Bench instead measures *proactive* persona-sensitive influencing — can a language agent persuade a profiled simulated user in three realistic interaction scenarios. Simulated clients are endowed with explicit user profiles derived from dialogue histories, and 10 frontier LLMs are evaluated. Two findings stand out: (a) even SOTA models leave substantial room for improvement in persuasion, and (b) giving the agent access to the client's profile yields an average +18.24% performance gain, quantifying the value of user-specific context for an agent that has to drive, not just react.

**Theme:** 2 — Multi-agent / agentic-system evaluation (LLM-as-agent ↔ LLM-as-simulated-user; proactive personalized agents).

**Relevance:** The only paper in the 48 h window that genuinely lives in the Theme-2 space — a benchmark for an *active*, persuasion-oriented agent role and a quantitative result on how much user-context conditioning is worth. Useful both as an eval target and as evidence for design choices in personalized agent stacks.

**Link:** https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.02754

## 4. World Models Meet Language Models: On the Complementarity of Concrete and Abstract Reasoning
**Authors:** Yucheng Zhou, Wei Tao, Yiwen Guo (Tencent)

**Summary:** The authors argue that world models (concrete visual rollouts of possible futures) and MLLMs (abstract rule/goal-level reasoning) are complementary but currently uncoordinated — rollouts can be visually plausible yet task-incorrect — and frame the missing capability as "controlled concrete reasoning": a model that learns *when* to invoke visual simulation, *whether* a rollout is credible, and *how* it should update the answer. They release two human-verified benchmarks (VRQABench, OpenWorldQA) and propose PF-OPSD, a training scheme in which ground-truth future videos are used only as teacher-side privileged context to score on-policy concrete-reasoning trajectories of a student that never sees the true future at test time. PF-OPSD beats the baseline by ~10.6–10.9 points on the two benchmarks and is reported to be more robust under noisy or conflicting rollouts.

**Theme:** 3 — Core LLM advances (planning/reasoning; teaching an LLM-style reasoner to gate and integrate an external simulator).

**Relevance:** A clean Theme-3 contribution on agentic reasoning over external simulators, with a non-trivial training trick (privileged-future on-policy self-distillation) and concrete benchmark wins. Directly relevant to anyone building agents that have to decide *when* to call a generator/simulator subroutine.

**Link:** https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.03603

## 5. Small RL Controller, Large Language Model: RL-Guided Adaptive Sampling for Test-Time Scaling
**Authors:** Runpeng Dai, Tong Zheng, Rui Liu, et al.

**Summary:** Test-time scaling buys reasoning accuracy at the cost of compute and latency; existing adaptive-sampling tricks either lean on heuristics or assume specific answer distributions. The authors reformulate adaptive sampling as an MDP and train a tiny RL controller — using only statistics over final answers, light enough to train and deploy on CPU — to decide at each round whether to stop or draw another sample, jointly trading off correctness, latency, and cost. They also give a Lagrangian-relaxation reading that makes the budget constraint explicit, and report improved correctness/round/sample trade-offs against strong baselines like ASC and ESC.

**Theme:** 3 — Core LLM advances (test-time scaling for reasoning; lightweight RL controller for compute allocation).

**Relevance:** A Theme-3 pick that targets the *control-plane* of reasoning models rather than their weights — and does so with a CPU-deployable controller that doesn't require touching the base model. Useful in agent stacks where inference budget is a first-class constraint.

**Link:** https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.03102
